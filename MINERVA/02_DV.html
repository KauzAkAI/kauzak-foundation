The idea of 43 workers’ compensation was to prevent employees from instituting private lawsuits against employers 44 based on common law. In exchange for providing those benefits, employers generally cannot be sued in 45 the court for causing injuries. 46 47 The Future … 48 We are just a few years into the 21st Century and have already seen sweeping changes with the 49 abolishment of the Department of Labor and Employment Security, the Division of Safety, and the Special 50 Disability Trust Fund. If you have a dispute regarding the risk 36 classification codes, contact the National Council on Compensation Insurance (NCCI) at (800) 622- 37 4123 38 39 Q: Does the injured worker pay any part of my workers' compensation insurance premium? 40 A: The law is very specific on this point. It is the employer’s responsibility to pay the entire premium for 41 workers' compensation insurance coverage. 42 43 Q: What kinds of employee injuries are covered? 44 A: The law covers all accidental injuries and occupational diseases arising out of and in the course and 45 scope of employment. This includes diseases or infections resulting from such injuries. The law also 46 covers death resulting from such injuries within specified periods of time. Even if you do not think an 47 injury is covered, you must still file the First Report of Injury or Illness (DWC-1) with your insurance 48 carrier within seven days of your first knowledge of the accident/injury for determination of 49 responsibility. 50 51 Q: What injuries are not covered? 52 A: The law does not provide compensation for the following conditions: 53 • A mental or nervous injury due to stress, fright, or excitement. 54 • A work-related condition that causes an employee to be afraid or dislike for another individual because of 55 the individual's race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, or handicap. 56 • "Pain and suffering" have never been compensable in Florida, nor is it compensable in any other state. 57 • The employer may not sue an injured worker for causing a catastrophe nor can the injured worker sue the 58 employer for their injury. This trade-off makes it possible for injured workers to receive immediate medical 1 care, at no cost to the injured worker, without any consideration for who was at fault, the employer or the 2 employee. In civil law, negligence must be established through litigation before any compensation is 3 awarded. [F.S. 440.02(1)] 4 5 Q: Can an employer be liable for double compensation? 6 A: An employer can be liable for double compensation if a minor child is injured while employed in 7 violation of any of the conditions of the child labor laws of Florida. The employer alone, not the 8 insurance carrier, is liable for up to double the normal compensation as provided by the workers' 9 compensation law. To receive further information regarding the Child Labor Law, call the Child Labor 10 Office at (800) 226-2536. 11 12 Q: As a small business owner, can I be sued by an injured worker if I provide all the necessary care, light 13 duty work, and offer to retrain the employee? 14 A: Under the provisions of F.S. 440, an injured worker has two years from the date of the accident to file 15 a petition for benefits with the Division of Administrative Hearings. If an employer provides benefits and 16 return to work options, that should be sufficient to meet the ultimate goal of returning an injured worker 17 to gainful employment. 18 19 However, an employer/carrier's definition of "necessary care" and that of an injured worker may differ. 20 When that happens, the injured worker has no remedy except to file a petition for benefits and have a 21 judge of compensation claims determine whether the benefits that are being provided are sufficient, or if 22 additional benefits not being provided are required by Florida law. If the employer is providing benefits, all 23 expenditures must be reported to the employer's workers' compensation insurance carrier for statistical 24 purposes. 25 26 Q: What in the system would prevent an injured worker, who wanted to leave their employer anyway, from 27 claiming to be hurt, waiting out the treatment, still claiming to be hurt and then trying to settle? 28 A: By law, pain or other subjective complaints alone, in the absence of objective relevant medical 29 findings, are not compensable. However, sometimes these types of claims do occur, and they are 30 sometimes settled by insurance carriers for a nominal amount of money to rid the employer/carrier of a 31 nuisance case. 32 33 Q: Is compensation payable if an employee refuses to use a safety appliance like a hard hat, safety 34 goggles or observe a safety rule? 35 A: Compensation will still be paid, but indemnity benefits (partial wage replacement) may be reduced by 36 25% if the employee knew about the safety rule prior to the accident and failed to observe the rule, or 37 if the employee knowingly chooses not to use a safety appliance which the employer has directed him 38 to use. [F.S. 440.09(5))] 39 40 Q: Will becoming a drug-free workplace save me money on my workers’ compensation insurance 41 premium? 42 A: If you implement a drug-free workplace program in accordance with the criteria set forth in F.S. 43 440.102, you may be eligible for a 5% premium credit on your workers’ compensation insurance 44 premium. In addition to the premium credit, having a workers' compensation drug-free workplace 45 program may make your workplace safer, resulting in fewer accidents, which may reduce your 46 workers’ compensation costs. 47 48 Q: Under the workers’ compensation drug-free workplace program, can I conduct random drug testing of 49 my employees? 50 A: In addition to the situations in which testing is mandatory, the law does not prohibit a private employer 51 from conducting random testing or any other lawful testing of employees. A public employer may 52 institute random testing of employees in "safety sensitive" or "special risk" occupations. 53 54 PROHIBITED ACTIVITIES 55 56 F.S. 440.105(2) Prohibited Activities; Reports;
