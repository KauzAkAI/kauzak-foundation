Simply put, impedance is a number measuring the 44 resistance an electric circuit or wire to the flow of an AC electrical current. 45 46 When impedance becomes too great for a given flow of AC electrical current the result is excessive heat 47 being built up in the wire or circuit. It stands for Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter and will 39 not clear a short circuit. 40 41 It monitors the hot and neutral for any imbalance in current. The imbalance can be on the hot or neutral. 42 43 Often consumers do not understand them, and they also think it has an effect when someone is caught 44 between the hot and neutral. A GFCI ONLY detects ground faults and that needs to be stressed. A 45 ground fault is when EITHER the hot or neutral current is diverted from the circuit conductors through an 46 alternate path. 47 48 If for example, a person contacts the hot or neutral wire and conducts a flow of electricity to earth, the 49 current transformer shown in the diagram (shown on the next slide) senses a difference of electrical flow 50 between the hot and neutral. When this flow exceeds the 5-mA threshold, the device “trips” and 51 disconnects the hot and neutral conductor from the circuit to protect the person. 52 53 Notice in the diagram, the test button simulates the same type of scenario previously mentioned and 54 should be tested once a month, according to the manufacturer's instructions. 55 1 2 3 GFCI vs. AFCI 4 5 Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters (GFCI) help prevent burns, electric shocks, and electrocution. A 6 GFCI has sensors that measure the current going out and the current coming back in. Normally, the 7 current is balanced as it goes out and comes back in. 8 However, if the current is out of balance, something is wrong. The electric current has contacted a 9 human, or somewhere else it should not be. 10 11 The GFCI senses this and instantly shuts down the circuit, stopping the flow of electricity. Since water is 12 an excellent electric conductor, GFCIs are important in areas where water and electricity could meet, 13 such as bathrooms, kitchens, laundry rooms and garages. 14 15 GFCI vs. AFCI 16 17 Arc Fault Circuit Interrupters (AFCI) help prevent electric fires. An AFCI should never be installed in 18 place of or as a substitute for a GFCI. 19 20 Electricity can leak out of damaged or decaying wires and start a fire. These fires spread quickly in the 21 wiring behind walls. Electric fires cause more damage than other types of fire and are twice as deadly. 22 AFCIs sense that electricity is leaking from the electric system and shut electricity off before overheating 23 happens. 24 25 Basically, GFCIs prevent shocks, and AFCIs prevent fires.
